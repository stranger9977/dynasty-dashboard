Introduction
“The film doesn’t lie.”

Actually, it does. All the time.

If film were pure truth, prospect evaluation wouldn’t be subjective.
Teams wouldn’t disagree. Analysts wouldn’t argue. Entire draft
rooms wouldn’t split over the same player.

And yet, they do. Every year.

“The numbers don’t lie.”

Actually, they do. All the time.

Numbers don’t tell stories — they simplify them. They strip
context. They smooth uncertainty into something that feels
precise, even when it isn’t.

Film lies by convincing us we’re seeing the whole picture.

Numbers lie by convincing us we don’t need one.

Like Jimmy Eat World at the turn of the century, the answer lies
somewhere in the middle.

The thing is, I, JJ — hey, nice to meet you — am far more
comfortable on one side of the prospect evaluation coin.

I can get down with numbers.

But they do lie. Because they’ll never tell the whole story.

That’s OK, though. As long as you know that going in.

What you’re about to read won’t give you the answers. It’ll just get
you closer to them.

It’s hard to believe, but this is the fifth iteration of the Late-Round
Prospect Guide. If you’ve been here before, you probably know

Over the last half decade-plus, I’ve built prospect models for
running backs, wide receivers, and tight ends designed to
estimate future fantasy football performance.

And they’ve worked.

The Zachariason-Adjusted Prospect Model — or ZAP Model —
saw Malik Nabers as the best wide receiver in his draft class when
consensus was locked onto Marvin Harrison Jr. It flagged Isiah
Pacheco as a value before the masses even knew who he was. It
projected Khalil Shakir as a potential impact slot player despite his
Day 3 draft capital. It adored Jaxon Smith-Njigba.

All of it came through math. Through numbers.

This year is no different. There are additions and adjustments

But at the center of it all is the same guiding principle it’s always
been: The goal isn’t to be certain. It’s to be directionally right.

Prospect models aren’t meant to totally replace evaluation.
They’re meant to challenge it. To nudge us away from bad bets,
toward better ones, and closer to the right questions — even when
the answers remain frustratingly unclear.

In a process this uncertain, that edge matters.

The ZAP Model
Overview
The goal of the ZAP Model hasn’t changed, but the way it gets
there continues to evolve.


       At its core, it’s designed to estimate how
       effective an offensive player will be for fantasy
       football purposes over the first three years of
       his NFL career.
The reasoning is simple: by the end of Year 3, we usually have
a pretty good idea of who a player is. Late-career breakouts
do happen, but they’re rare enough that they shouldn’t drive
a prospecting process. If we’re trying to identify future fantasy
contributors, the first three years are where the signal is strongest.

That naturally raises the question of how early-career success
should be defined.

You could look at cumulative fantasy points. You could focus on
average points per game. You could even weigh peak seasons
more heavily than consistency. There’s no single correct answer.
But, over time, I’ve landed on a metric that balances upside,
sustainability, and fantasy usability.

That metric is called B2S.

(Yes, another acronym. I’m sorry. Or you’re welcome.)

B2S stands for “Best Two Seasons.” For each player, we look at his
first three NFL seasons and identify his two best years in points
per game in point-per-reception formats. Those two seasons are
then averaged together.

Imagine a wide receiver who averages 11.0 PPR points per game


Late-Round Fantasy Football: 2026 Prospect Guide
Jump To: ZAP Model | Year 2 Model                                        7
as a rookie, 12.0 points per game in Year 2, and 15.0 points per
game in Year 3. Under B2S, the rookie season would be discarded,
and the 12.0 and 15.0 seasons would be averaged, resulting in a
B2S of 13.5.

This approach rewards players who reach meaningful fantasy
relevance, even if it takes time, and it avoids over-penalizing
prospects who start slow but ultimately develop into usable assets.

Of course, points per game can be misleading in small samples.
A player who averages 21 points per game across three contests
before suffering a season-ending injury probably shouldn’t be
treated the same as someone who sustains that level of production
across a full season.

To account for that, a season only qualifies for B2S if the player
appears in at least seven games. If he fails to meet that threshold,
the season is treated as a zero for B2S purposes.

And since the final week of the NFL season is often filled with
backups and teams playing for very little, Week 18 is removed
from the B2S sample as well. In other words, B2S is only
evaluating relevant fantasy weeks.

So, going back to our example: if that wide receiver played only
five games in his second season — the one where he averaged
12.0 points per game — but cleared the seven-game threshold in
his rookie (11.0 PPR points per game) and third (15.0) years, then
his B2S wouldn’t be 13.5. Instead, the injury-shortened season
would be pushed aside, and the rookie and third-year numbers
would be averaged, producing a B2S of 13.0.

That’s B2S. And that’s what the ZAP Model is attempting to
predict.

So how does the model get there?

Over the years, I’ve worked extensively with college production,
athletic testing, and draft data. Through that process, I’ve
developed separate prospect models for running backs, wide
receivers, and tight ends, each tailored to the nuances of the
position.

But building a model is easy. Proving it’s useful is not.

       and generate a score. If that score doesn’t
       meaningfully improve our understanding —
       if it doesn’t outperform something we already

Draft capital is always the first benchmark I test against. If a

Thankfully, that hurdle is cleared. Every positional ZAP Model
included in this guide is more predictive of B2S than draft capital
alone.

Much more.

As for the player pool that’s being evaluated, each model
analyzes all players who were drafted or invited to the NFL
Combine since 2011. There’s typically significant overlap there,
but each year a handful of prospects slip through one channel or
the other.

Even though the ZAP Models outperform draft capital on their

The goal here isn’t to predict where a player should be drafted in

There’s no medal awarded for ignoring draft capital during

As a result, draft capital is the single most important input in

The Wide Receiver ZAP Model
The ZAP Models are adjusted every offseason. Not to
retroactively fix misses — players from the most recent class
aren’t even eligible for testing yet, since they haven’t completed
three NFL seasons.

The updates happen for a simpler reason: the landscape keeps
changing. New data becomes available. Better ways of analyzing
that data emerge. And over time, my own approach to prospect
evaluation evolves.

This year is no different. The wide receiver model has changed.

A couple of offseasons ago, I introduced the concept of Breakout
Score to the prospect models. Breakout Age is a commonly
referenced metric in analytically driven prospect evaluation, and
it used to play a role in the ZAP Model as well.

But I’ve always had issues with it.

For those unfamiliar, Breakout Age represents how old a player
was when he first reached a certain Dominator Rating threshold.
Dominator Rating, to anyone unaware, measures the percentage
of a team’s receiving production a player accounted for during
his college career.

The intuition behind Breakout Age is sound. Early production
matters. When a player earns a significant role at a young age,
it’s usually a sign that he’s simply better than his peers.

If a 10-year-old can play Canon in D on the piano the same way
a 17-year-old can, odds are that when both pianists turn 20, the
younger one is going to be ahead.

(I use that analogy every year in this guide, and it’s not going
anywhere. Sorry.)

Breakout Age isn’t useless, but it is flawed.

Its biggest issue is that it’s far too binary. A player either clears




Late-Round Fantasy Football: 2026 Prospect Guide
Jump To: ZAP Model | Year 2 Model                                       11
the threshold or he doesn’t. There’s no room for nuance.

A wide receiver can fall just short of the breakout mark and be
treated the exact same way as someone who barely contributed
at all. The metric doesn’t care how close you were. It only cares
whether you crossed an arbitrary line.

Another way to think about it: imagine evaluating wide receivers
solely by whether they reached 500 receiving yards in a season.
A player with 499 yards and a player with 20 yards would be
treated identically, despite clearly not being the same caliber of
prospect.

That’s the fundamental problem with Breakout Age. It captures
when a player produced, but not how much he actually produced
when explaining that breakout.

And when you’re trying to separate good prospects from great
ones, that distinction can be important.

That frustration with Breakout Age ultimately led to Breakout
Score.

Like Breakout Age, Breakout Score is designed to capture age-
adjusted collegiate production. Unlike Breakout Age, it doesn’t
rely on a single threshold. And it doesn’t lean on Dominator
Rating, a metric that comes with its own set of limitations.

Instead, Breakout Score evaluates a player’s receiving yards per
team pass attempt at multiple points throughout his collegiate
career, adjusting for both age and competition level.

Let me explain.

Receiving yards per team pass attempt is exactly what it sounds
like: a player’s receiving yards divided by the number of pass
attempts his team recorded. The ZAP Model uses a prorated
version of the stat, so if a player misses some time, that’s
accounted for. However, if he appears in fewer than six games
in a season, that year is excluded from the Breakout Score




Late-Round Fantasy Football: 2026 Prospect Guide
Jump To: ZAP Model | Year 2 Model                                    12
calculation.

The “adjusted” portion of Breakout Score comes from two
inputs: strength of schedule and age.

For strength of schedule, the model uses team-level data from
Sports Reference. Those numbers are translated into a multiplier
and applied to a player’s receiving yards per team pass attempt
for each season. The idea is simple: production against stronger
competition should carry more weight than production against
weaker opponents.

It’s easier to rack up numbers at South Alabama than it is at
Alabama.

Once that schedule-adjusted number is established, Breakout
Score then evaluates it through the lens of age. The younger a
player is when he posts a strong schedule-adjusted receiving
yards per team pass attempt, the more favorably he grades
within the metric.

In short, Breakout Score rewards early, efficient production
without forcing players into arbitrary buckets.


       Breakout Score shows a much stronger
       relationship to B2S than Breakout Age, and it
       greatly improves the effectiveness of the ZAP
       Model.
The wide receiver model also accounts for rushing production.
That might sound a little strange at first, but when a receiver is
used as more than just a pass-catcher, it’s usually a signal of
genuine talent. Coaches don’t manufacture touches for players
they don’t trust. If a wideout is getting handoffs or schemed
runs, it’s because the offense wants the ball in his hands as often




Late-Round Fantasy Football: 2026 Prospect Guide
Jump To: ZAP Model | Year 2 Model                                     13
as possible.

Within the model, rushing production is capped. The goal isn’t
to turn gadget usage into a cheat code. Instead, modest rushing
totals act as a proxy for versatility and, more broadly, for being
good at football.

The final production-related input in the wide receiver model is
best-season adjusted fantasy points per game, a new addition
this year. The concept is straightforward: it takes a player’s best
collegiate season in fantasy points per game (with a minimum
of six games played) and adjusts that output for both age and
strength of schedule.

That covers the production side of the wide receiver model. But
several inputs have nothing to do with what a player did on the
field on Saturdays.

Early-declare status is the most notable.

It’s a concept that’s been debated in dynasty circles for years,
and the logic behind it isn’t all that different from metrics like
Breakout Age. When a player chooses to leave school early and
enter the NFL Draft, it’s usually because he’s good enough
to do so. NFL teams are showing interest, and the player is
responding.

If that’s the case, he’s probably pretty good at playing the
position.

That’s why early-declare status carries weight in the model.

There’s also a practical consideration. Early-declare receivers
have fewer collegiate seasons on their résumé. That puts them
at a slight disadvantage in pure production metrics, since they
don’t get the benefit of padding their profile with a fourth or fifth
year of college production.

Most early-declare wideouts still post strong age-adjusted
numbers, of course. But older prospects can sometimes inflate
their statistical profiles by simply sticking around longer. Early




Late-Round Fantasy Football: 2026 Prospect Guide
Jump To: ZAP Model | Year 2 Model                                       14
declaration helps correct for that imbalance.

The common question people have with early-declare status
these days relates to name, image, and likeness (NIL) deals.
Players can make real money while staying in school, and they
can buy themselves more time to develop before stepping into
the NFL.

And it’s true: wide receivers are leaving early at a lower rate than
they were before. From 2023 through 2025, for example, 15% of
the wide receivers in the ZAP Model were early declares. In the
12 years prior, that number was closer to 26%.

But a lower early-declare rate doesn’t mean early declaration
suddenly stopped mattering. It just means the environment
changed.

If anything, NIL raises the bar for leaving early. When staying in
school comes with both financial incentives and the comfort of
a familiar situation, the players who still choose to declare early
are often doing so because the NFL feedback is strong enough
to outweigh those benefits. In other words, the “why” behind an
early declaration hasn’t gone away — the decision is just being
made under different circumstances.

So no, early-declare status isn’t something to ignore. It’s
something to interpret correctly. It’s not a trump card, and it’s
not a deal-breaker. It’s a signal — one piece of a larger puzzle —
and the model treats it that way.

There’s also Teammate Score. When a wide receiver plays
alongside other pro-caliber players, it matters. Intuitively, that
makes sense: it’s harder to command a large share of an offense
when you’re competing for targets with legitimate NFL talent.

Teammate Score isn’t perfect, but it’s built using the draft
capital of a player’s college teammates from the current draft
class and recent seasons. As a result, a receiver’s ZAP score can
shift slightly as the NFL Draft unfolds, depending on where those
teammates are selected. The changes are usually small, but the
signal is real.

The final non-production-related input in the wide receiver ZAP
Model is size.

Jump To: ZAP Model | Year 2 Model                                      15
Smaller wideouts aren’t incapable of posting useful fantasy
seasons, but they do tend to face steeper odds when it comes
to reaching true ceiling outcomes. There are situations where a
player will be dinged for being on the smaller side, not because
success is impossible, but because history shows it’s harder to get
there.

So, to recap, the wide receiver model incorporates Breakout
Score, rushing production, best-season adjusted fantasy points
per game, early-declare status, Teammate Score, and size. Draft
capital is then layered on top — and, once again, it’s the single
most important piece of the puzzle — to create a player’s ZAP
score.

And no, athletic testing from the NFL Combine isn’t part of the
model.

When people hear that prospect models exist, they often assume
they’re driven primarily by NFL Combine numbers. That makes
sense on the surface — bigger, faster, stronger sounds better than
the alternative.

But in practice, athleticism is already doing a lot of its work
through draft capital.



       Truly unathletic wide receivers rarely get
       selected highly in the first place.
And if a player completely bombs the Combine, that usually shows
up elsewhere: weaker draft capital, a thinner production profile, or
both.

Beyond that, wide receivers win in different ways. Rome Odunze
doesn’t succeed the same way Ladd McConkey does. Asking a
model to enforce one athletic archetype would ignore the reality of
how the position actually functions.

If anything, there’s a reasonable argument that athleticism at wide

it doesn’t need to be debated. The information is already baked in.


The Running Back ZAP Model
We just spent a lot of time talking about wide receivers. So
naturally, let’s talk about running backs and the thing that matters
most for them as they transition from college to the NFL.

Receiving.

Last year, it felt a bit like an episode of Maury when I revealed
that the running back ZAP Model didn’t include a single rushing
statistic. Every production-related input was tied to pass-catching.

That wasn’t an oversight. It was intentional.

And after more testing, that hasn’t changed much in 2026.

Receiving still rules all at the running back position.

Look, these prospect models aren’t built to help me run an NFL
franchise. They’re built to help us win at fantasy football. Draft
capital remains the foundation of everything here, but when it
comes to identifying when and where to diverge from a player’s
draft slot, receiving metrics consistently tell the most useful story
for running backs.

One of the core inputs in the running back model is reception
share, a metric I’ve leaned on for years. It’s simply the percentage
of a team’s completions that went to a running back, prorated
to account for missed time. Reception share tends to be most
informative at the top of the draft, where it works alongside draft
capital to separate similar profiles, but it still carries signal deeper
into drafts as well.

In last year’s version of the model, Breakout Score was also used
at running back, not just at wide receiver. As a reminder, that
metric is derived entirely from receiving yards per team pass
attempt. Receiving yards per team pass attempt remains part
of the running back model today, but it’s no longer bundled into




Late-Round Fantasy Football: 2026 Prospect Guide
Jump To: ZAP Model | Year 2 Model                                          17
Breakout Score for the position. Instead, the model now looks at
a player’s best season in age- and schedule-adjusted receiving
yards per team pass attempt.

What’s new this year is that some rushing production has been
allowed back into the conversation.

Some.

The model now incorporates age- and schedule-adjusted total
yards per team play, calculated in the same way as receiving
yards per team pass attempt, but using all offensive yards and all
team plays. This gives rushing production a voice without letting it
drown out the more predictive receiving signals.

I alluded to this a second ago, but one of the more meaningful
tweaks in 2026 is how these production metrics are weighted
depending on where a player is selected in the NFL Draft. For
example, a late-round pick’s yards per team play may matter more
for his ZAP score than his reception share, whereas the opposite is
often true for early-round selections.

Each of these production metrics are useful on their own. But they
become far more powerful once we know exactly where a player is
drafted.

Keep that interaction in mind as you read through this year’s
running back profiles.

Collegiate production isn’t the only thing the running back ZAP
Model is concerned with.

We’ve looked at some age-related production metrics at the
position, but age itself is a factor here, too. It’s certainly not
everything, and NIL deals are shifting this landscape a tad, but
older running backs are usually worse bets to make compared to
younger ones. Production will always trump age, though.

Teammate Score is an input in the running back model, too, just
like the wide receiver one. This seems a little counter-intuitive,
but there’s actually less weight given to Teammate Score for
running backs compared to wideouts – you’d figure a split
backfield with a legitimate pro would boost a running back’s score



Late-Round Fantasy Football: 2026 Prospect Guide
Jump To: ZAP Model | Year 2 Model                                      18
more than it does. But, in the end, the ZAP Model does still care
about teammate competition, even if it’s a little less than at wide
receiver.

The final two non-production inputs in the running back ZAP
Model are also the most controversial: size and Speed Score.

Running back size appears to matter less and less in today’s NFL.
We watch players like James Cook, De’Von Achane, Jahmyr Gibbs,
and Kyren Williams put up big fantasy numbers on a weekly basis,
and none of them fit the traditional mold of a “big” running back.

Because of examples like those, it’s easy to dismiss the idea that
size matters at all. But they’re better viewed as exceptions than
proof that the rule no longer exists.

It’s also important to be clear about how the ZAP Model treats
size. Weight is not evaluated in a vacuum.

Take Jahmyr Gibbs. He entered the league at 199 pounds, which
led to a bizarre fixation on an arbitrary 200-pound threshold. Some
film evaluators understandably pushed back, pointing out how silly
it is to treat a one-pound difference — something that could be
dictated by what a player ate earlier that day — as meaningful.

The ZAP Model agrees. A strict cutoff like that is bad analysis.

But Gibbs is on the smaller side. That fact simply doesn’t matter
much when everything else checks out. Draft capital. Receiving
profile. Yards per team play. When all of those boxes are checked,
size becomes a footnote, not a deal-breaker.

That’s why Gibbs owns one of the highest ZAP scores the model
has ever produced. The same logic applies to De’Von Achane.
Yes, he’s small, but he also posted an absurd college production
profile. The model accounts for both.

Size isn’t irrelevant — larger backs, all else equal, have historically
been more reliable fantasy producers — but it’s nowhere near
sufficient on its own to forecast NFL success. And it never
operates alone within the model.




Late-Round Fantasy Football: 2026 Prospect Guide
Jump To: ZAP Model | Year 2 Model                                         19
The other debated input is Speed Score.

Speed Score, originally developed by Bill Barnwell, recognizes
that not all 40-yard dashes are created equal. Running a 4.40 at
200 pounds isn’t the same thing as running a 4.40 at 230. When
adjusted for weight, faster backs generally produce better fantasy
outcomes.

Generally.

Once again, the mistake is treating a single metric as definitive.
Kareem Hunt is a good example. His Speed Score was
unimpressive, but the rest of his profile was strong. He entered
the league before his 22nd birthday, had solid size, and posted
excellent age- and schedule-adjusted production numbers. As
a result, he still graded well in the ZAP Model despite lacking
straight-line speed.

If it were entirely up to me, running back size wouldn’t matter
much at all. But it does matter to NFL teams. Smaller backs are
less likely to be handed massive workloads, and in fantasy football,
volume still drives everything.

The good news is that smaller backs can absolutely offset that
disadvantage with elite production and receiving profiles. The
model allows for that flexibility.

That’s the running back ZAP Model, though.

On the production side, it uses reception share, receiving yards
per team pass attempt, and yards per team play. On the profile
side, it incorporates age, weight, Teammate Score, and Speed
Score. Layer draft capital on top, and you’ve got the full picture.

Well — maybe not the entire picture.

Draft capital is obviously a key part of the model, but it’s
calculated a little differently than it is at wide receiver and
tight end. After doing some investigating last year, I found that
combining NFL Mock Draft Database’s consensus mock drafts
with actual draft capital gave the running back ZAP Model more




Late-Round Fantasy Football: 2026 Prospect Guide
Jump To: ZAP Model | Year 2 Model                                      20
signal — it made it more predictive.

In practice, that means the model uses a slight blend of real and
projected draft capital — about an 83/17 split between actual
draft capital and consensus projections.

Why would this help?

For one, it introduces a data point that isn’t tied to a single team’s
decision. And it may simply be that the collective football brain —
the analysts, scouts, and evaluators contributing to those mock
drafts — does a pretty good job identifying running back talent.
Interestingly, that same signal doesn’t appear to hold as strongly
for wide receivers and tight ends.

This might sound like a big change, but it really isn’t. In most
cases, projected and actual draft capital aren’t drastically
different.

But in the name of transparency, I wanted you to know.


The Tight End ZAP Model
Over the last couple of years, I’ve been building a tight end version
of the ZAP Model. And it hasn’t been easy.

The results have been solid — you saw some of that in last year’s
guide — but tight end is a different animal. Unlike wide receivers,
tight ends don’t run as many routes. There are fewer players
putting up flashy receiving totals. They block more. And NFL
teams draft them not just to catch passes, but to function as an
extension of the offensive line.

In other words, what we’re trying to solve in fantasy football isn’t
always what an NFL team is trying to solve.

That disconnect makes prospecting at the position more difficult.
Draft capital, for example, doesn’t carry quite the same weight for
tight ends as it does for running backs and wide receivers.

Like the other ZAP Models, this one predicts B2S — a player’s
best two-season average across his first three NFL years. And
like the others, it looks at every tight end who was drafted or
invited to the NFL Combine since 2011.

But this model works a little differently.

Instead of primarily leaning on draft capital and adjusting from
there, the tight end model focuses on how three areas interact:
draft capital, athleticism, and collegiate production.

Draft capital is straightforward — it’s where the player was
selected. Athleticism, in this model, is height-adjusted Speed
Score. It’s traditional Speed Score, but adjusted for height, and it
carries more weight here than it does in the running back model.

On the production side, we’re looking at Breakout Score,
calculated similarly to the wide receiver model through age- and
schedule-adjusted receiving yards per team pass attempt.

Technically, the model also incorporates best-season receiving
yards per team pass attempt along with height and weight, but
those three core areas are the easiest way to understand what’s
going on.

Here’s the key difference with tight ends: athleticism
fundamentally changes what draft capital means.

Take Jordan Reed as an example. Reed entered the NFL as
a below-average athlete but was a Day 2 pick with legitimate
college production. The model really likes him. When a tight end
lacks high-end athleticism, early draft capital becomes more
informative. It suggests the team saw something else — route-
running, spatial awareness, technical refinement — that testing
numbers don’t capture. A less athletic player who still produced
in college is likely winning in ways that raw speed doesn’t show,
and if a team invests meaningful draft capital in that profile, that
investment carries real signal.

Teams won’t always be right. But when they draft a mediocre
athlete early, and that player has strong production, the model
recognizes that combination as meaningful.

On the flip side, the model doesn’t automatically reward
tight ends just for being elite athletes. We often assume that
a freakishly athletic Day 2 tight end must have a big fantasy
future because a team “has plans” for him. But if the college
production profile is weak, that player will take a significant hit
in the ZAP Model. Athleticism is part of the puzzle, not the entire
picture.

There’s a balance between investment, traits, and production,
and that’s what the model is trying to capture.

A good example of this balance is the gap between Hunter Henry
and Michael Mayer. Henry has a ZAP score of 85.6 in the model.
Mayer’s is 73.0. That’s a meaningful difference, even though the
two players were nearly identical across most inputs. Both were
selected 35th overall. Their height and weight were almost the
same. Their best-season yards per team pass attempt differed by
just 0.01. Their Breakout Scores were essentially no different.

translatable traits.

The team wasn’t just drafting traits. The team was drafting a
complete profile.

production.

In this metaphor, I’m the meteorologist. You’re coming to me for

But I’m not interested in just throwing a number on the screen

responsibility — not yours — to explain what that forecast

That’s why the ZAP Model got a bit of a makeover this offseason.

Staying in the spirit of transparency, it’s important to point out
that the ZAP score you’ve historically seen on a player’s profile
isn’t the raw number the model generates. Behind the scenes,
the model produces a score based on its inputs that lives in a
relatively narrow range — roughly the low 20s to high 40s at
wide receiver, and single digits to the high 20s at running back.

Those raw numbers aren’t particularly intuitive on their own.
Without context or scaling, they’re difficult to interpret.

In the past, I addressed this by converting scores into
percentiles. That worked fine at first, but over time, it became
clear that percentiles introduced their own problems. They force
the data into a linear distribution and can obscure meaningful
gaps between prospects who shouldn’t feel interchangeable.

Draft Capital Delta
You may have noticed a glaring outlier in the wide receiver hit-rate
table — a Benchwarmer who posted a B2S north of 20 PPR points
per game.

That player was — is — Puka Nacua.

The model labeled him at the top of the Benchwarmer category.

Holy L.

Or… maybe not.

Draft capital anchors ZAP scores by design. And historically, Day 3
wide receivers — players drafted on the third day of the NFL Draft
— simply haven’t been very fantasy relevant. Since 2011, the list of
wideouts selected after Pick 100 who’ve averaged at least 14 PPR
points per game in B2S is extremely short: Puka Nacua, Tyreek
Hill, Amon-Ra St. Brown, and Martavis Bryant. Bryant didn’t even
provide production in Year 3.

That’s the list.

Nacua was drafted 177th overall. No sane model is going to treat
a player with that type of draft capital as a top-tier prospect. And
if a model does rank a Day 3 wide receiver near the top of a class,
there’s almost certainly some overfitting happening.

So yes — a low ZAP score for a late-round pick is common. But that
doesn’t mean the model is telling us to completely ignore those
players.

That’s where Draft Capital Delta comes in.

   Every prospect has two things: a ZAP score, and an implied
   score based solely on his draft capital. A first overall pick
   might carry a draft-capital score near 100. An undrafted player
   would be close to zero.

   Draft Capital Delta measures the difference between those two
   values. In effect, it asks: How does the model view this player
   once we strip away what the NFL told us?

   In Nacua’s case, his ZAP score came in at 40.5. Based on his
   draft position, his score “should’ve” been closer to 33.9.

   That seven-point gap is meaningful, especially for a Day 3 pick.
   It tells us that, while his profile wasn’t pristine, there were still
   positive indicators — a respectable Breakout Score, some
   rushing involvement, and enough underlying production to
   separate him from a typical 177th-overall selection.

   Draft Capital Delta buckets players into three risk tiers: low
   risk, neutral, and high risk. Low-risk players are those the
   model likes more than their draft capital would suggest. High-
   risk players are the opposite — prospects whose ZAP scores
   lag behind how the dynasty market is likely valuing them.

   As you’d expect, low-risk players outperform their expected
   B2S at a much higher rate than high-risk players.

   It’s not foolproof. There will always be exceptions. But Draft
   Capital Delta adds valuable context — especially when you’re
   deciding whether a late-round prospect is worth taking a swing
   on, or whether an early pick carries more downside than the
   market realizes.

Key Terms
ZAP Score: The score given to an incoming rookie in the ZAP Model. It’s
based on the factors outlined in the ZAP Model Overview section of this
guide. For pre-draft scores, the draft capital input is based on projected
draft capital from NFLMockDraftDatabase.com. All scores range from 0
to 100.

ZAP Score Category: Every player’s ZAP score coincides with one of
seven categories, which were talked about earlier. Those categories will
be listed on every player’s prospect page.

Breakout Score: The score given to a wide receiver and tight end for his
age-adjusted production. This is based on yards per team pass attempt.
All scores range from 0 to 100.

B2S: The ZAP Model attempts to predict how well a player will do in
fantasy football across his first three years in the league. In doing this, it
tests against the average of a player’s top-two seasons in PPR points per
game during his first three years as a pro. This is referred to as B2S (Best
Two Seasons).

Draft Capital Delta: The diﬀerence between a player’s ZAP score versus
where he was drafted in the actual NFL Draft. A very low Draft Capital
Delta will be displayed as a “high risk” proﬁle. A very high one will be
shown as a “low risk” proﬁle. Most players will simply be “neutral”. Draft
Capital Deltas will be published in the post-draft version of the guide.

Speed Score: A concept developed by Bill Barnwell to help appropriately
weight 40-yard dash times based on a player’s size.

Statistical Comparables: Players in history who appear to have similar
analytical profiles to the player being analyzed. This is based on things
like size, age, Breakout Score, and other production metrics.

 Prior to this draft class, only one ZAP Model running back — Martez Carter — entered the

 This year, we have two. One of them is Seth McGowan.

 Does age matter? Yes. The model accounts for it, and historically, older prospects have
 produced weaker outcomes. But context is needed. College football has shifted dramatically
 in recent years. Transfer rules changed. The pandemic extended eligibility. NIL deals
 altered incentives. As a result, we’re seeing older backs more frequently.

 Over the last two draft classes (excluding this one), 10 running backs either attended
 the NFL Combine or were drafted at age 24 or older. From 2011 through 2023, there were 11
 total. Last year alone, RJ Harvey, Woody Marks, and Jacory Croskey-Merritt were all 24 or
 older at selection.

 Adjusted for draft capital, the hit rates for these older prospects haven’t been
 disastrous of late.

 As long as the production profiles were strong.

 McGowan’s profile isn’t bad — it’s just uneven. He began at Oklahoma in 2020 before being
 dismissed following legal issues, though the charges were ultimately dropped. After time
 away from the game, he resurfaced at Butler Community College in 2023, then transferred to
 New Mexico State in 2024. There, he logged 152 carries in a backfield that also featured
 2026 draft hopeful Mike Washington. More importantly for his analytical outlook, McGowan
 posted a career-best 16.3% reception share that year.

 That season did a lot of the heavy lifting for his pass-catching profile, showing at
 least some ability to function on passing downs. The question is sustainability. When

The Year 2 Model
Overview
There’s a video game called Rocket League that’s been around
for a minute, and it’s still wildly popular. At its core, it’s simple:
car soccer. You control a car and try to smash a ball into your
opponent’s goal.

I’ve played it on and off for years. It’s one of those games you
can jump into for 20 or 30 minutes, knock out a few five-minute
matches, and move on.

When you queue up for an online game, Rocket League pairs you
with teammates and opponents who have similar skill ratings. In
theory, everyone on the field should be roughly equal.

In practice? Not always.

You can play with friends, but if you queue solo for a two-on-two or
three-on-three match, you’re paired with random teammates.

And those players can be total wildcards.

Within the first minute or two, you usually figure it out. Maybe
your teammate is clearly outmatched. Maybe they’re excellent,
but their rating is suppressed by bad luck or poor teammates in
previous games.

Your expectations adjust quickly once you see real information.

The same thing happens in fantasy football.

Prospects enter the league with a set of expectations based
on what we knew about them coming out of college. Then they
play actual NFL games. Suddenly, we have new data that either
reinforces or challenges those original assumptions.

The question is: how much should those expectations change?

At both running back and wide receiver, the Year 2 Model
combines a player’s prospect score, certain rookie-season
production inputs, and his rookie-year fantasy points per game
to generate a Year 2 score. That score is designed to recalibrate
expectations using both pre-draft information and what we
actually saw on the field.

In previous iterations, the model focused on a player’s best
season in either Year 2 or Year 3. This year, that window has been
expanded through Year 4.

Why? Because it gives us a larger, more stable sample to work
with. Expanding the window allows the model to better capture
delayed breakouts, injury interruptions, and uneven early-career
development — all things that can distort shorter evaluations.

As you’d expect, the Year 2 Model does a much better job
predicting a player’s next three seasons than rookie-year points
per game alone.

Just like the ZAP Model, the Year 2 Model can be summarized
through probability tables. Players are grouped into the same
categories you’ve already seen — Legendary Performer, Elite
Producer, and so on — allowing us to clearly see how often
different tiers of players go on to hit specific B2S thresholds.

That’s not because the ZAP Model is flawed. It’s because we

NFL competition — we’ve seen it.

We’re deeper into the Rocket League match now. We’ve watched
our teammate play. The skill rating makes more sense.

One thing you may notice when looking at the tables above is that
the Year 2 Model behaves a little differently at running back than
it does at wide receiver. Every elite Year 2–4 wide receiver lands
in the Weekly Starter tier or above. At running back, though, a few
productive players slip through the cracks.

There are real football reasons for that.

Running back production is often dictated by coaching decisions.
If a staff wants a split backfield, it gets one. And for fantasy

purposes, split backfields are a problem — they lower ceilings and
introduce volatility.

A running back can have a strong rookie season, but if his team
decides to change roles, add competition, or rethink usage, that
production doesn’t always carry forward.

Wide receivers are different. There are more of them on the field.
More routes. More chances to earn targets. When opportunity
expands, talent tends to win out. That’s ultimately what these
models are trying to capture — talent — and it’s why wide receiver
outcomes are generally more predictable.

Running back is also a more replaceable position. That doesn’t
mean backs aren’t valuable; it means teams know they can find
usable production later in the draft or in free agency. When supply
is high, demand drops — and that creates instability.

That instability shows up clearly in the data.

From 2011 through 2023, there were six running backs selected
after Pick 100 who averaged at least 12 PPR points per game as
rookies (minimum seven games played, relevant fantasy weeks
only). When looking at those players’ performances from Year 2
through Year 4, every single one of them peaked as a rookie. None
improved their fantasy production after Year 1.

Ask yourself why that might be.

From a general manager’s perspective, a Day 3 rookie having a big
season doesn’t automatically mean he’s a long-term solution. Was
it real talent? Or was it circumstance? Scheme? Injuries elsewhere?

Front offices will likely second guess themselves, wondering why
that running back fell to the third day of the draft in the first place,
regardless of his rookie-season production.

Because running backs are relatively easy to replace — and
relatively inexpensive — teams often introduce competition
quickly. That competition suppresses long-term fantasy ceilings,
especially for later-round players.

In other words, rookie-year running back production is incredibly
volatile.

Journey Comparables
At some point, every fantasy analyst ends up asking the same
question:

Have we seen this before?

When something weird happens in the league, we ask it. When a
player posts an absurd stat line, we ask it. When a team wins nine
straight games by a field goal or less, we ask it.

The Year 2 Model helps answer that question through Journey
Comparables.

You’ve already seen statistical comparables tied to the ZAP Model.
Journey Comparables are different. Instead of comparing player
attributes as they enter the league, they compare players based
on how their evaluation changes.

Specifically, Journey Comparables look at a player’s ZAP score
alongside his Year 2 Model score and identify historical players
who followed a similar path — from pre-draft expectations to post-
rookie-season reality.

They ignore play style, size, athletic testing, and role. The only
thing that matters is the journey: how a player was viewed entering
the league, and how that view changed after Year 1.

History doesn’t repeat itself perfectly.

But in fantasy football, it rhymes often enough to matter.

a player’s ZAP score. All scores range from 0 to 100.

categories will be listed on every player’s Year 2 profile page.

 Emeka Egbuka’s rookie season is difficult to summarize without sounding dramatic. Because
 the production split is genuinely extreme.

 Over the first five weeks of the season, Egbuka averaged more than 20 PPR points per game.
 From that point forward, his average fell to just 8.3. That’s not a gradual decline –
 that’s a cliff.

 When researching his profile, the first thing I wanted to know was whether this kind of
 early-season surge followed by a prolonged drop-off actually shows up in historical data.
 Have we seen rookie wide receivers start this strong and then fade this dramatically?

 The answer? Not really.

 Among Year 2 Model wide receivers, a drop like this simply hasn’t happened. Puka Nacua
 averaged over 20 PPR points per game through Week 6 as a rookie, but finished the season
 closer to 16. Calvin Ridley is a better comparison – he averaged 19.7 PPR points per game
 through Week 4 of his rookie year before falling to 10.7 the rest of the way. Even then,
 that decline wasn’t nearly as severe as what we saw from Egbuka.

 That’s why it’s important to zoom out and look at the full sample. Egbuka entered the
 league with a strong ZAP score, and his full-season rookie numbers were impressive: a 1.75
 yards per route run rate, a target share just shy of 24%, and the second-most first downs
 among wideouts in his draft class.

 Egbuka also lands in excellent company with his Year 2 Model score of 77.7. Within the Year
 2 Model database – prior to this most recent draft class – we’ve seen eight wide receivers
 with Year 2 scores within three points of Egbuka’s who also posted a yards-per-route-run
 rate north of 1.60. Those players? Allen Robinson, DJ Moore, DK Metcalf, Terry McLaurin,
 Chase Claypool, Amon-Ra St. Brown, Rashee Rice, and Zay Flowers.

 Not bad.

 We shouldn’t call Egbuka bust-proof – just take a look at that first Journey Comparable —
 but landing in the Elite Producer category gives him strong odds to be at least a WR2 in
 fantasy football moving forward. Especially now that Mike Evans is gone. And especially
 when his 58% catchable targets rate – a 4th percentile number – regresses favorably.

 To put that another way, it’s likely that Sampson’s line wasn’t doing him any favors this

 Still, the overall profile doesn’t scream high ceiling.

 the aforementioned Jacory Croskey-Merritt, James Robinson, and fellow Rutgers alum Isiah

 ceiling.

 forced per attempt, and Next Gen Stats had his rushing yards over expected per attempt

 Devin Neal more or less did what the ZAP Model suggested he would do as a rookie.

 Was he great? Probably not.

 Was he playable? Absolutely.

 Thanks to injuries to Alvin Kamara and Kendre Miller, Neal found himself operating as New
 Orleans’ lead back for stretches of the season. He logged a 70% snap share three times, and
 in those games, he averaged a respectable 12.1 PPR points per contest.

 Neal entered the league projecting as a potential three-down back — just not a dominant
 one. His collegiate profile suggested competence across the board without a true standout
 trait. And that’s exactly what we saw in 2025. When given the opportunity, he handled early
 downs, ran routes at a high clip, and stayed on the field in passing situations. In those
 three high-usage games, his route participation exceeded 70%, leading to an 11% target
 share.

 In other words, the coaching staff trusted him.

 It tells us that if injuries strike again, Neal is capable of absorbing a near three-down
 workload. It may not be explosive or hyper-efficient, but it’s usable volume.

 Efficiency-wise, though, nothing jumped off the page. His success rate was low and similar
 to Kendre Miller’s. His 10-plus-yard run rate lagged behind most of his rookie classmates.
 And his receiving efficiency was below the norm.

 He didn’t separate. But he also didn’t crater.

 And that’s really the story here. Neal profiles as a stabilizer — the type of back who can
 keep an offense on schedule and deliver replacement-level fantasy production when called
 upon. He’s unlikely to become a league-winner, but he’s also unlikely to disappear.

 In dynasty, that archetype has value. He’s a high-end depth piece with contingent upside —
 the kind of player you’re glad to roster when the inevitable running back injuries hit.

 Not extraordinary. But very rosterable.

Football isn’t solved. It never will be.

If it were, there’d be no reason to do this. No reason to build models. No
reason to debate landing spots or argue over breakout ages. No reason
to care about a 21-year-old running back’s target share.

But this game lives in the gray area.

Humans are playing it. Coaches are making decisions. Front offices are
guessing. Injuries happen. Situations change. And we’re all trying to
make sense of it — to reverse engineer a sport built on chaos.

That’s what makes prospecting so addicting. There’s always room for
improvement.

This guide isn’t about certainty. It’s about probability. It’s about stacking
small edges over time. It’s about having a process you trust when you’re
on the clock and the group chat is losing its mind.

We’ll get things right. We’ll get things wrong. That’s part of it.

But we’ll keep refining. Keep questioning. Keep iterating. Because the
edge isn’t in being perfect — it’s in being thoughtful.

Thanks for letting me obsess over this stuff for another year.
